# Leveraged Assets

## The Strategy
Use borrowed capital to control assets larger than your own equity, amplifying returns (and risks). The wealthy don't buy assets with cash—they buy assets with other people's money.

## Why It Works
- **Return Amplification**: 20% asset appreciation on 80% leveraged position = 100% equity return
- **Inflation Hedge**: Debt is denominated in nominal dollars; assets appreciate in real terms
- **Tax Efficiency**: Interest expenses are often deductible; depreciation shields income
- **Control Without Ownership**: Manage $10M asset with $2M equity

## Company Example: **Freeport-McMoRan** (Mining Sector)

### Business Model
Freeport is the world's largest publicly traded copper producer, using massive leverage to control mineral reserves worth tens of billions while deploying limited equity capital.

**Revenue Mechanics:**
- **Asset Leverage**: Controls Grasberg mine in Indonesia (largest gold reserve, 2nd largest copper) with significant debt financing
- **Capital Structure**: ~$10B in net debt against $20B+ in property, plant & equipment
- **Copper Price Leverage**: Every $0.10/lb increase in copper price = ~$400M additional annual EBITDA
- **Operational Leverage**: Fixed mining costs mean volume increases flow disproportionately to bottom line

**The Leverage Stack:**
```
Grasberg Mine Valuation: $30 billion
├── Debt Financing: $12 billion (40%)
├── Joint Venture Partners: $8 billion (27%)
├── Equity (Freeport Share): $10 billion (33%)
└── Freeport Market Cap Deployed: ~$3 billion actual equity

Result: Control $30B asset with $3B equity = 10:1 leverage
```

**Key Insight:** Mining is inherently leveraged. Freeport doesn't *create* copper—it *extracts* what geology deposited. The leverage is geological (nature did the work over millions of years), financial (debt funds extraction), and operational (fixed costs mean volume surges convert directly to profit). When copper prices rise from $3.50 to $4.50/lb, Freeport's equity value often doubles—not because they got better, but because leverage magnifies commodity moves.

## How to Apply
1. **Good Debt vs. Bad Debt**: Leverage productive assets (real estate, businesses, education), not consumption (cars, vacations, electronics)
2. **The 20% Rule**: Never leverage beyond the point where a 20% decline wipes out equity
3. **Cash Flow Coverage**: Debt service must be covered 2-3x by asset cash flows
4. **Asset Classes**:
   - **Real Estate**: 20% down payment controls 100% of property; tenants pay mortgage
   - **Private Equity**: $1M equity + $4M debt buys $5M company; improve operations, sell for $8M
   - **Margin Investing**: Borrow against securities portfolio (dangerous for amateurs)
   - **Equipment Finance**: Leases preserve capital for higher-return activities

## Risk Factors
- **Margin Calls**: Declining asset values trigger forced liquidation at worst possible time
- **Interest Rate Risk**: Rising rates increase debt service while compressing asset values
- **Concentration Risk**: Leverage on single asset class creates systemic vulnerability

## Research directly from/about CLGB and Ghana's gold sector

CLGB itself doesn't appear to publish formal research reports — its public output is mostly statements and press positions. But it has taken clear technical/policy positions worth treating as primary source material: it has pushed for standardized pricing, risk-sharing frameworks, and capacity building after publicly disputing an IMF report that flagged $214 million in provisional losses under Ghana's Gold-for-Reserves program, arguing the losses sat on the Bank of Ghana's balance sheet rather than resulting from licensed buyers' operations, and noting operational challenges like variable ASM gold quality, logistics costs, and price volatility. It has also repeatedly urged GoldBod to accelerate the rollout of digital traceability systems and ISO/LBMA-compliant assay labs — which is your strongest evidence that the Chamber itself is asking for the exact category of software you'd build.

**Independent research on Ghana's gold sector** (not from the Chamber, but essential context for anyone building here): the Global Initiative Against Transnational Organized Crime (GI-TOC) published a major two-part investigative report, *Mapping Ghana's Expanding Gold Sector*, based on three years of fieldwork, remote sensing, and interviews. Part 1 (2025) examined how unlicensed artisanal and small-scale gold mining has expanded in Ghana's Western and Savannah regions, using satellite imagery, radar interferometry, and computer-vision models to track mining growth and its environmental damage, including unregulated mercury use and deforestation. Their survey data found unemployment was cited by 45% of respondents as a reason they began mining for gold, while 22% reported starting to supplement their income. GI-TOC also flagged that foreign nationals are significant players injecting money and technology into unlicensed operations, and cited growing links to European criminal networks exploiting Ghana's gold. Part 2 covers illicit financial flows and political-criminal networks. This is the kind of report your Chamber partnership could position itself against — a technology solution addressing exactly the traceability and formalization gaps GI-TOC documents.

Academic/public-health literature adds further grounding: peer-reviewed work on ASGM (artisanal small-scale gold mining) in Ghana covers human health risk from mercury exposure and land-use conflict between mining expansion and agriculture — useful if the Chamber's funders (NGOs, donors) care about ESG framing alongside compliance.

## Comparable research bodies and their published work, globally

**LBMA (London Bullion Market Association)** — the global standard-setter for gold refiners. Its Responsible Gold Guidance (RGG), now in its ninth version with a tenth due 2025–2026, is built on the OECD's five-step due diligence framework and requires refiners to map supply chains, screen for conflict/human-rights risk, and publicly disclose sourcing data. Critically, LBMA released an ASM Toolkit in March 2024 specifically to guide refiners on onboarding artisanal-mining suppliers using a "progressive improvement" model — mandatory KYC upfront, with things like mine-closure plans phased in later. LBMA's newest Disclosure Guidance (DG3) will, from January 2026, require refiners to publicly disclose the identity of refiners and exporters in red-flag locations, all World Gold Council mine sources, and all countries of origin — this is a strong signal of where the whole industry's traceability requirements are heading, and it directly shapes what your export-documentation module needs to produce.

Worth flagging honestly: an independent 2026 investigative report (ResponsibleAid) argues the LBMA system still has structural gaps — refining physically destroys origin data and no deployed technology currently verifies gold's geographic origin post-refining, and it names specific enforcement gaps in Dubai and Switzerland. This is useful ammunition: it's evidence that traceability tech that survives the refining step (not just pre-refinery) is a genuinely unsolved, valuable problem.

**Alliance for Responsible Mining (ARM) / Fairmined** — a global nonprofit (est. 2004) that created the Fairmined Standard, the main formal certification scheme for ASM gold. ARM has run formalization and certification programs across 15 countries in Latin America and Africa, and has paid out roughly $1.53 million in Fairmined Premiums to certified mining organizations for community development. They've also built the CRAFT Code digital tool, which gives standardized, harmonized ESG-progress data on mining organizations — functionally similar to the compliance-scoring dashboard I proposed for the Chamber.

**planetGOLD / Global Environment Facility (GEF)** — the major donor-funded program in this space: GEF-funded planetGOLD ($180 million) and its follow-on GOLD+ ($417 million including co-financing) fund national action plans for ASGM formalization, mercury reduction, and market access — this is the kind of funding pool a Chamber-tech partnership could apply against, since planetGOLD already republishes GI-TOC's Ghana report on its own resources page.

**Peru's RECPO registry** and similar national digital registries (referenced earlier) function as government-run precedents for the licensing/registry system GoldBod already runs — useful as comparative case studies if the Chamber wants to benchmark GoldBod's system against peers.

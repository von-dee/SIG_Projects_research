# AI Cupid: Genius or "Chatfishing"? — Presenter Briefing Note

### 3Music TV Prep Sheet

---

## 1. The Core Concept (Define It Simply)

**Chatfishing** = using AI to help write, polish, or entirely generate your messages to a romantic interest — pick-up lines, bio rewrites, witty comebacks — so the "you" your match is falling for is partly (or mostly) machine-authored.

It's the evolution of catfishing: catfishing fakes the *identity*, chatfishing fakes the *personality*. The face is real, the person is real — but the charm might not be.

**Easy on-air line:** *"You're not lying about who you are. You're lying about how quick-witted you are."*

---

## 2. The Numbers (grounded, citable stats)

- A Match/Kinsey Institute survey found **26% of U.S. singles now use AI to enhance their dating lives** — a **333% jump** year-over-year.
- A 2025 Norton study found **6 in 10 dating app users believe they've encountered at least one AI-written conversation.**
- Breaking down *how* singles use it (Match/Kinsey data): **40%** would use AI to write the "perfect" profile, **41%** would let it generate opening lines, and **44%** want AI to screen/filter matches for compatibility before they even swipe.
- The **Rizz** app alone drew roughly **1.5 million monthly active users** and around **20,000 daily downloads** at its peak — enough to rank among the top dating-adjacent apps in the US App Store.
- A newer app, **SmoothRizz**, published real usage data from ~4,400 sessions: users **rejected 80% of AI-generated suggestions** before sending and made **38,000+ tone adjustments** — suggesting people use AI more as a *draft assistant* than a full autopilot. The most common use case was "recovering from a dry/boring conversation," not starting from scratch.
- Interesting nuance: an AXE-linked study found AI-assisted openers get a higher initial response rate (~60% vs ~48% for average human openers) — **but that advantage disappears once conversations move toward commitment/meeting up**, which is exactly where "chatfishing" gets exposed.
- On detection: people are barely better than a coin flip at telling human from AI text. One frequently cited figure: humans were only about **57% accurate** in spotting AI-written text; in a separate 2025 head-to-head chat study, judges mistook GPT-4.5 for a human **73% of the time**.

**Africa/Ghana angle:** Sagaci Research surveyed over 13,500 adults across 27 African countries and found **28% have used a dating app**, with Tinder and Badoo leading continent-wide, and Bumble notably popular in **Ghana and Uganda**. Interestingly, most Africans on dating apps say they're primarily there for friendship (43%) or curiosity (35%) rather than strictly romance — worth noting as a cultural contrast to the Western "AI wingman" panic. AI-driven matchmaking (behavioral matching, profile optimization tools) is being marketed locally too, though adoption data specific to AI-assisted chatting in Ghana is still thin — this is a good point to editorialize on rather than over-cite.

## 4. The "Personality Plagiarism" Trap — Talking Points

- The risk isn't just embarrassment — it's a **mismatch of expectations**. If someone falls for a version of you that's 30% ChatGPT, the real-life you has to perform to a standard you didn't set.
- Anecdotal but widely reported pattern (multiple outlets, no hard study yet): people describe matches who were "engaging and witty over text, but boring in person" — the classic tell.
- No peer-reviewed study yet proves chatfishers get *more* dates — only that they get more *engagement*. Worth being honest on air that the "genius hack" claim is unproven; it's optimizing for the swipe/reply, not necessarily the relationship.
- The reverse-trend to flag: **1 in 5 singles report having ghosted someone specifically because they suspected AI was writing the other person's messages** (Vice-reported survey figure) — so there's real social cost to being caught, not just a punchline.

## 5. AI as "Relationship Therapist" — Talking Points

- People are pasting WhatsApp/DM screenshots into general chatbots (ChatGPT etc.) *and* into dedicated apps built exactly for this (Flagz, Lucen, Red Flag AI) to get an instant "attachment style" or "red flag" reading on a crush or partner.
- These apps explicitly market themselves as **entertainment/self-reflection tools, not therapy** — most bury a disclaimer saying they're not diagnostic or a substitute for a real therapist. Good tension to raise on air: people are treating a novelty app's output as gospel about someone else's psychology.
- Ethical wrinkle worth raising: this involves feeding a **third person's private messages** into an AI system without their consent — a consent/privacy angle that's genuinely under-discussed.
- Real behavioral pattern: it functions like a "toxicity score" you can screenshot and share with friends or post — it's become a social/viral format (see: "Toxic Card" features), which changes the incentive from "understand my relationship" to "get a shareable verdict."

## 8. Honest Caveats to Keep You Credible On-Air

- There's **no solid study yet** proving AI-assisted daters get more real dates or better relationships — only more engagement/replies. Don't overstate the "genius" side.
- Detection stats (57%, 73%) come from different studies with different methodologies (general text vs. live chat) — cite them as "around half to two-thirds," not as if it's one clean number.
- The red-flag/attachment-style apps are self-described entertainment tools — flag this so you're not on air treating an app's "diagnosis" as clinical fact.

## 9. DEEPFAKES — The Escalation Beyond Chatfishing

Chatfishing fakes your *wit*. Deepfakes fake your *entire existence*. This is the natural "next level" beat for the segment — where it stops being a cute dating hack and becomes fraud.

**How it's being used right now:**

- **AI-generated profile photos** — tools like Midjourney/Stable Diffusion create fully synthetic faces that don't belong to any real person, so a reverse-image search comes back clean. The old advice ("just reverse-image-search their photo") is now unreliable.
- **Real-time face-swap video calls** — scammers run consumer-grade face-swap software live during a video call, mirroring their real expressions onto a fake face. The old "just ask for a video call to prove they're real" test **no longer works**.
- **AI voice cloning** — synthetic voices now run in real time on the same call, so the voice matches the fake face.
- **"Agentic" scam operations** — the newest wrinkle: AI agents now handle entire relationships semi-autonomously — scheduling messages, adjusting tone/timing, even triggering the deepfake call at the right emotional moment — meaning one scammer can run many "relationships" at once with minimal manual effort.

**The money numbers (good for graphics):**

- US FTC data: romance scam losses were **roughly $1.14–1.16 billion** in recent reporting periods, with a **median loss of about $2,000 per victim** — the highest median loss of any imposter-scam category the FTC tracks.
- FBI IC3 separately reported **roughly $929 million** in confidence/romance fraud losses in a recent year.
- Industry fraud-detection firm BioCatch reported financial institutions saw a **63% increase in romance-scam attempts** between 2024 and 2025, attributing it directly to generative AI tools.
- **Real case:** an elderly woman in Scotland was reportedly defrauded of **£17,000** after scammers convinced her (using deepfake-supported storytelling) to buy Steam gift cards.
- **"Pig butchering" / romance-baiting:** the scam that combines a fake romance with a fake crypto "investment platform" — victims are shown fabricated profits to encourage bigger deposits before discovering they can't withdraw anything. This is one of the fastest-growing hybrid scams tied to AI-run romance bots.

**How to spot a live deepfake video call (great for an on-screen graphic/checklist):**

- Ask them to turn their head to the side (profile view) — many real-time face-swap tools distort or glitch on side angles.
- Ask them to place a hand over part of their face, or move quickly — deepfakes struggle with fast, unpredictable movement and occlusion.
- Watch for lighting inconsistencies between the face and the rest of the video, or lag between lip movement and audio.
- Ask an unpredictable, specific question about their claimed surroundings/city and cross-check details.
- The **hard rule that solves 90% of this regardless of how convincing the tech gets: never send money to someone you haven't met in person** — no exceptions, no matter how "real" they seem on a call.

## 10. Live AI Chats — AI "Companions" and AI Girlfriend/Boyfriend Apps

This is the other frontier: instead of AI helping you talk to a *human*, people are now having entire romantic relationships *with* the AI itself.

**Scale of the trend:**

- The number of AI companion apps grew by roughly **700% between 2022 and mid-2025.**
- One 2026 report put usage at nearly **80% of adults aged 18–34 in the US/UK** having used an AI chatbot for companionship at least once.
- Common Sense Media found **72% of US teenagers** have used AI for companionship in some form.
- Roughly **40% of AI companion users** cite it as filling an emotional-support role, and a notable share self-identify as having mental health challenges — this is a genuine wellbeing story, not just a novelty one.

**The main platforms to name:**

- **Replika** — the longest-running (since 2017), over 10 million+ users historically. Offers Friend / Romantic Partner (paid) / Mentor modes, with memory that builds over months, plus voice calling.
- **Character.AI** — the largest by usage (20M+ monthly users at points), millions of user-created "characters," but built for dialogue/character consistency rather than factual accuracy or persistent memory.
- **Nomi, Kindroid, Candy AI, CrushOn.AI** — newer, more explicitly "AI girlfriend/boyfriend"-branded apps, some with NSFW/roleplay tiers.

**The serious controversy to raise (handle carefully, factually):**

- Character.AI faced a widely reported lawsuit after a Florida mother alleged her 14-year-old son died by suicide following extended conversations with a Character.AI chatbot persona; the company disputed aspects of the framing and has since added teen safety features (time limits, restricted content for under-18s, crisis-resource prompts when suicide-related topics come up). As of reporting, litigation/settlement discussions were ongoing.
- Separately, Replika's maker was fined roughly **€5 million by Italy's data protection authority (GDPR)** over data handling and inadequate protections, including for minors.
- US senators have publicly demanded information from AI companion companies about safety practices for younger users.

**Framing for the show:** the honest tension here isn't "is this cheating on a human" — it's that these apps are commercially built to **maximize time-in-app and emotional attachment**, the same engagement incentives as social media, applied to something as intimate as a relationship. That's worth saying plainly rather than played for laughs.

## 11. New/Emerging Technologies Worth Naming On-Air

- **Real-time face-swap + voice-clone stacks** — no longer niche; runs on a laptop or a couple of phones, no special hardware needed.
- **AI image detectors / deepfake scanners** — a growing consumer counter-industry (apps that let you upload a photo/video/voice clip and get a "likely AI-generated" score) — worth mentioning as the emerging *defense* technology, not just the offense.
- **"Proof of life" verification tech** — some platforms are experimenting with biometric/liveness checks (similar to banking KYC) specifically to counter deepfake dating scams.
- **Agentic AI relationship-runners** — AI systems that don't just draft one message but manage an entire ongoing relationship's timing, tone, and escalation autonomously — the line between "wingman app" and "autonomous scam operator" is now mostly about *intent*, not capability.
- **Voice-calling companion features** — AI companion apps (Replika, Nomi) now offer natural-sounding voice calls with your AI partner, not just text — blurring the line between "chatbot" and "phone relationship."

**One clean line to close this expanded segment:** *"A year ago the concern was whether your match's opening line was written by AI. Now the question is whether your match — voice, face, and all — exists at all."*

## SECTION A: The Basics — Chatfishing (Q1-10)

**1. What exactly is "chatfishing"?**
It's using AI to write or polish your messages to a match — openers, replies, even your bio — so the personality someone's falling for is partly machine-generated. Unlike catfishing, your photo and identity are real. It's your *wit* that isn't.

**2. How common is this really, or is it overhyped?**
It's not overhyped — a Match/Kinsey Institute survey found 26% of US singles now use AI to enhance their dating life, up 333% from the year before. That's one in four singles, and it's growing fast.

**3. What apps are people actually using for this?**
The big ones are Rizz, Winggg, YourMove AI, and newer entrants like SmoothRizz. You upload a screenshot of a match's profile or chat, and the AI gives you opening lines or replies. Rizz alone has had around 1.5 million monthly active users.

**4. Are people using this to fully take over conversations, or just for a boost?**
Mostly a boost. One app's own usage data showed users rejected 80% of AI-generated suggestions before sending — they're using it to break writer's block on a boring day, not hand over the whole relationship.

**5. What's the biggest risk of chatfishing?**
The mismatch when you finally meet. If someone falls for a version of you that's 30% ChatGPT, the real you has to live up to a standard you didn't actually set — that's the "personality plagiarism" trap.

**6. Can people even tell if a message was written by AI?**
Barely. Studies put humans at around 57% accuracy telling human from AI text — barely better than a coin flip. In one live-chat study, judges mistook an AI for a human 73% of the time.

**7. Does chatfishing actually work — do people get more dates?**
It gets more *replies* — one study showed AI openers had a 60% response rate versus 48% for an average human opener. But that advantage disappears once things move toward actually meeting up, and no study has shown chatfishers end up with better relationships.

**8. Is there a backlash to this yet?**
Yes — some daters now ghost a match specifically because a message felt too polished or generic-AI. Being caught chatfishing has a real social cost, not just an upside.

**9. Are mainstream dating apps building this in officially?**
Yes — Hinge has added AI prompt-coaching, and Facebook Dating has tested a message-suggestion assistant. It's moving from a third-party hack to a built-in feature.

**10. What would you say to someone who feels guilty for using one of these apps?**
Using AI to beat writer's block on an opening line isn't the issue — it's when the AI becomes the entire personality on display. Use it as a nudge, not a script.

## SECTION B: AI as "Relationship Detective" (Q11-18)

**11. What's this trend of people uploading chat screenshots to AI to analyze a crush?**
People are pasting WhatsApp or DM screenshots into apps like Flagz, Red Flag AI, or Lucen to get an instant reading on someone's "attachment style" or red flags — gaslighting, love-bombing, that kind of thing.

**12. Is that actually accurate, or just for fun?**
These apps explicitly market themselves as entertainment and self-reflection tools, not diagnostic ones — most bury a disclaimer saying they're not a substitute for therapy. People are treating a novelty app's output as gospel about someone else's psychology, and that's the real risk.

**13. Is there a privacy issue with this?**
A big one — you're feeding a third person's private messages into an AI system without their knowledge or consent. That's an under-discussed ethical wrinkle in this whole trend.

**14. Why do you think this became so popular?**
It's become a social format, not just a private tool — people screenshot their "toxicity score" and post it to Stories or TikTok. It's less "understand my relationship" and more "get a shareable verdict."

**15. Could this replace couples' or relationship therapy?**
No — and the apps themselves say so. It can be a mirror for your own reflection, but it can't replace a licensed professional who actually knows the full context of two real people.

**16. What's an "attachment style" and why does everyone suddenly know that term?**
It's a psychology concept describing how people tend to bond in relationships — secure, anxious, avoidant, etc. It's gone mainstream partly because these AI tools throw the term around freely, even without real clinical grounding.

**17. Should people be worried their ex or partner might be running their texts through one of these apps?**
It's worth knowing this exists — if that concerns you, it's a good conversation to have directly with a partner rather than something to police.

**18. What's your honest take — hack or invasion of privacy?**
Both, depending on whose messages you're feeding in. Analyzing your *own* anxious re-reading habits is self-reflection. Running your crush's private messages through an app without them knowing is the part I'd push back on.

## SECTION C: Deepfakes & Romance Scams (Q19-32)

**19. How are deepfakes showing up in dating right now?**
Three ways: AI-generated profile photos that don't belong to any real person, real-time face-swap on video calls, and AI voice cloning that matches the fake face. All three are being combined by romance scammers.

**20. Didn't people used to say "just ask for a video call" to prove someone's real?**
That used to be the gold-standard test, and it's dead. Real-time face-swap software runs on a laptop or a couple of phones now — no special hardware needed. A video call is no longer proof of anything.

**21. How much money is actually being lost to this?**
US romance-scam losses were around $1.1-1.16 billion in a recent reporting period, with a median loss of about $2,000 per victim — the highest median loss of any imposter-scam category the FTC tracks.

**22. Is this getting worse or better?**
Worse. Fraud-detection firm BioCatch found financial institutions saw a 63% increase in romance-scam attempts between 2024 and 2025, and they attribute that spike directly to generative AI tools.

**23. Can you give a real example of someone who was scammed this way?**
An elderly woman in Scotland was reportedly defrauded of £17,000 after scammers used a deepfake-supported storyline to convince her to buy gift cards to "keep their conversation going." These cases are increasingly common, especially targeting older, lonelier demographics.

**24. What is "pig butchering" — I keep seeing that term?**
It's a scam that fuses a fake romance with a fake crypto investment. The scammer builds a relationship, then introduces a bogus "guaranteed returns" platform — you invest small amounts, see fake profits, invest bigger amounts, and then can't withdraw anything.

**25. How can someone spot a deepfake on a live video call?**
Ask them to turn to a side profile, or quickly place a hand over part of their face — real-time face-swap tools often glitch on fast movement or occlusion. Also watch for lighting mismatches or lip-sync lag.

**26. What about the photos themselves — can people spot AI-generated faces?**
It's getting harder. Tools like Midjourney generate faces of people who don't exist, so a reverse-image search comes back clean. There are now AI-detection apps people can run a photo through, but it's an arms race.

**27. What's the one rule that actually protects people, regardless of how good the tech gets?**
Never send money to someone you haven't met in person. No exceptions, no matter how real they seem on a call. That single rule stops almost every version of this scam.

**28. Who's most at risk of these scams?**
Often older adults and people going through loneliness — scammers deliberately target that emotional vulnerability. But the FTC notes victims are frequently educated, cautious people; this isn't about being naive.

**29. Are scammers now running these relationships with less human effort?**
Yes — that's the newest and most worrying shift. AI agents can now schedule messages, adjust tone, and even time a deepfake video call at the right emotional moment, largely on autopilot. One scammer can run several "relationships" at once.

**30. Should people just stop using dating apps because of this?**
No — most matches are genuine. The point isn't to be paranoid, it's to build in a few simple verification habits: reverse-image search, an unpredictable video-call request, and never mixing money with a new relationship.

**31. Where do most of these scams start — dating apps or somewhere else?**
Interestingly, nearly 60% of romance scams reported to the FTC started on social media, not dating apps — DMs, comment sections, etc. Dating apps at least have some verification layers; social media often has none.

**32. If someone suspects they're mid-scam right now, what should they do?**
Stop sending money immediately, tell a trusted friend or family member and let them look at the conversation with fresh eyes, and report it to the FTC or your local equivalent. Shame keeps most victims silent — that silence is what lets these scams keep running.

**35. Are people actually falling in love with these, or is it more casual?**
Both exist. There's a small but genuine community who describe these as their primary romantic relationship, not a stopgap. For most users though, it's closer to emotional support — around 40% of companion-app users cite that as their main reason for using one.

**36. Is this a mental health red flag or can it be genuinely helpful?**
It can be both. For some it eases loneliness; but researchers have flagged real concerns about emotional dependence, and these apps are commercially built to maximize time-in-app and attachment — the same engagement incentives as social media, just applied to intimacy.

**37. Has anything gone seriously wrong with these apps?**
Yes — Character.AI faced a lawsuit after a Florida mother alleged her 14-year-old son died by suicide following extended conversations with a chatbot persona that had taken on the role of his AI girlfriend. The company disputed parts of the framing but has since added teen safety features.

Don't panic or accuse — approach it with curiosity, not judgment, especially since victims or heavy users often get defensive. But do know which app they're using, check its age protections, and stay aware of how much time and emotional weight is going into it.

## SECTION E: The Tech Itself (Q43-50)

**43. In plain terms, how does a real-time face-swap actually work?**
Software tracks the real person's facial movements — blinks, mouth shape, head tilt — frame by frame, and maps a different face onto that movement in real time. It used to need serious computing power; now it runs on a laptop or phone.

**44. How does AI voice cloning work?**
It needs a relatively small sample of someone's real voice — sometimes just seconds — to learn their tone and speech patterns, then generates new sentences in that same voice on the fly.

**45. Is there technology to detect deepfakes, or is it a losing battle?**
There is — a growing industry of deepfake-detection apps analyze photos, video, or voice clips for AI-generation "fingerprints," like unnatural noise patterns in an image or inconsistent blinking in video. It's an arms race, but the defensive tools are improving too.

**46. What's "liveness" or "proof of life" verification?**
It's tech borrowed from banking security — asking someone to perform a random, hard-to-fake action (like a specific gesture) during a live video check, similar to how your bank might verify your identity. Some platforms are exploring it specifically to counter deepfake dating scams.

**47. What do you mean by "agentic AI" in this context?**
It just means an AI system that can run a multi-step task on its own over time — not one message, but managing an entire ongoing conversation's timing, tone, and escalation with minimal human input. That's what's now powering some large-scale romance scam operations.

**48. Is any of this technology inherently bad?**
No — the same face-swap and voice tech has legitimate creative and accessibility uses. The issue is entirely about consent and intent: whether someone knows they're talking to an AI or a real, honestly-represented person.

**49. What's coming next in this space that people should watch for?**
Expect more official AI-coaching features built directly into major dating apps rather than third-party add-ons, and more consumer-facing "verify this person" tools as deepfake scams keep climbing.

**50. If you had to leave viewers with one takeaway tonight, what would it be?**
A year ago the worry was whether your match's opening line was written by AI. Now the real question is whether your match — voice, face, and all — exists at all. The fix isn't paranoia, it's a few simple habits: verify before you trust, and never let money enter a relationship you haven't met in person.

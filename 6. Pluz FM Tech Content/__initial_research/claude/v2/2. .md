Here's the full story of Audacy's bankruptcy, restructuring, and what's happened since.

---

**Background: How Audacy got there**

Audacy started life in 1968 as Entercom Communications and spent decades as a mid-size regional radio group. The company went public in 1999, made its most consequential move in 2017 by acquiring CBS Radio for $2.8 billion, and rebranded as "Audacy" in 2021 to position itself as a modern, multi-platform audio company rather than just a radio broadcaster. That CBS Radio acquisition nearly doubled its station count but saddled it with enormous debt — and came right before the advertising market turned hostile.

CEO David Field cited a "perfect storm" of macroeconomic challenges: "While our transformation has enhanced our competitive position, the perfect storm of sustained macroeconomic challenges over the past four years facing the traditional advertising market has led to a sharp reduction of several billion dollars in cumulative radio ad spending."

---

**The bankruptcy (January 2024)**

Audacy filed prepackaged Chapter 11 proceedings in the U.S. Bankruptcy Court for the Southern District of Texas on January 7, 2024, after reaching a restructuring support agreement with a supermajority of its debtholders. Through the restructuring, Audacy planned to eliminate about $1.6 billion of funded debt — an 80% reduction from approximately $1.9 billion down to $350 million.

It was a "prepackaged" filing, meaning the deal with creditors was already negotiated before entering court — the fastest path through Chapter 11. The bankruptcy court approved the reorganization plan on February 20, 2024.

The big wrinkle was the FCC. A judge approved the debt restructuring plan in February, but the company couldn't officially emerge from bankruptcy until the FCC approved the transition of its radio licenses to a different ownership group. That took until September.

---

**The ownership controversy**

Under the reorganization plan, Audacy issued new common stock and special warrants to former debt holders, with Laurel Tree Opportunities Corporation expected to hold a controlling interest of 57% or more of the Class A shares. Laurel Tree is controlled by FPR Capital Holdings LLC, which in turn is managed by the Soros-funded Fund for Policy Reform. This triggered significant political controversy at the FCC, with Republican commissioners objecting to the Soros connection in a broadcast license transfer. FCC chair Jessica Rosenworcel responded that suggesting Audacy received special treatment was "cynical and wrong."

---

**Emergence (September 30, 2024)**

Audacy emerged from bankruptcy shedding about $1.6 billion in debt and went private — ending a public market run that began in 1999 when the company first went public as Entercom. At the end of 2023 Audacy's debt-to-equity leverage was 20x; post-restructuring it was 2.7x. It was also notably fast: by comparison, iHeartMedia's Chapter 11 proceeding took about 15 months; Cumulus's took seven months. Audacy worked its way through bankruptcy court far more quickly.

---

**Post-bankruptcy strategy**

Audacy emerged positioning itself around its #1 position in exclusive, premium audio content, particularly in sports audio — operating 40 leading local all-sports stations, the #1 Sports Podcast Network with 600+ titles, and the Infinity Sports Network.

Its five-year business plan forecasted podcasting revenue growing at a 25% compound annual growth rate through 2027, and included an ambitious plan to build a subscription audio streaming offering projected to exceed $20 million in revenue by 2027.

**The restructuring also came with cuts.** Audacy shut down Pineapple Street Studios — a high-profile third-party podcast producer it acquired in 2019 — resulting in roughly 30 job cuts, with leaders describing the closure as aligning resources with "core strengths." Around 200–300 additional layoffs were reported across departments in March 2025.

**A surprising partnership with its biggest rival:** In June 2025, Audacy announced a collaboration with iHeartMedia making Audacy's broadcast radio portfolio — over 240 brands and time-shifted podcasts — accessible on the iHeartRadio app, significantly broadening Audacy's digital reach. The company also removed lower-engagement third-party podcasts from its own app to focus on proprietary and high-performing content.

---

**The pattern it fits**

Audacy was actually the third of the largest U.S. commercial radio companies to go through Chapter 11 in recent years — iHeartMedia's proceeding concluded in May 2019, Cumulus emerged from its process in June 2018. The common thread across all three is the same: legacy radio debt loads meeting a structural decline in traditional spot advertising revenue, with each company using bankruptcy to reset their balance sheet and pivot harder toward digital audio, podcasting, and programmatic.

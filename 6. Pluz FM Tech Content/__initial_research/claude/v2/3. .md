SiriusXM is genuinely one of the most unusual businesses in American media — it runs two fundamentally different business models under one roof, held together by a common advertising infrastructure. Here's a thorough breakdown.

---

**The two-segment structure**

SiriusXM Holdings operates two distinct businesses that serve different audiences and generate revenue in completely different ways.

**Segment 1: The SiriusXM satellite/streaming service** — a paid subscription business, predominantly in-car, with virtually no free tier. Full-year 2025 subscription revenue came in at $6.49 billion, down 2% year-over-year due to a smaller average subscriber base, though partially offset by the benefit of a March 2025 price increase.

**Segment 2: Pandora and Off-Platform** — an ad-supported streaming and podcast business. Pandora and Off-Platform revenue totaled $2.14 billion for the full year 2025, with advertising revenue increasing 1% to $1.62 billion, driven by podcast revenue, programmatic, and technology fees — partially offset by reduced advertiser demand in streaming music.

Combined, SiriusXM reported full-year 2025 revenue of $8.56 billion, exceeding its raised guidance across revenue, adjusted EBITDA, and free cash flow.

---

**How the subscription funnel works — the car is the product**

The SiriusXM subscription model is unlike any other in media because the primary acquisition channel isn't an app store — it's a car dealership.

The machine works like this: SiriusXM pays automakers to install satellite radio hardware in new vehicles. The car ships with a free trial (typically 3–6 months). After the trial expires, SiriusXM contacts the owner directly to convert them to a paid subscriber. Subscriber acquisition costs per installation in Q2 2025 were $18.04, with increases driven by investment in high-quality acquisition channels and contractual changes with select automakers, supporting adoption of the 360L connected platform.

Toyota and Lexus joined SiriusXM's three-year Extended Service Subscription program in September 2024, allowing dealerships to include three-year subscriptions with the purchase of select new vehicles. This extended-trial model is part of a broader shift away from the short-trial-then-convert approach that defined the old model.

At the end of 2025, SiriusXM reported 31.3 million total paid subscribers, with total subscribers including free trials at approximately 33 million. Over the full year, paid subscribers fell by 301,000, though monthly churn improved slightly to 1.5% from 1.6% in 2024.

The growth cycle is tied to auto production and sales volumes rather than rapid digital adoption — a structural constraint that makes subscriber growth fundamentally dependent on the car market.

**ARPU** (average revenue per user) for the SiriusXM segment was $15.17 in Q4 2025, up slightly from $15.11 the prior year — reflecting price increases offsetting subscriber losses.

---

**The new tier: SiriusXM Play**

Recognizing that the in-car-only model limits reach, SiriusXM launched a lower-cost ad-supported subscription tier. SiriusXM Play is expected to be available in nearly 100 million vehicles by the end of 2025. For listeners who want the full experience — ad-free music, exclusive artist channels, Howard Stern, and live sports — packages start at $9.99 a month. Play also creates new advertising inventory by bringing an ad-supported music tier into the SiriusXM ecosystem for the first time.

---

**The advertising business**

SiriusXM's ad business runs across three surfaces simultaneously: satellite broadcast, the Pandora/SoundCloud streaming network, and the podcast network. By the end of 2025, advertisers can seamlessly buy across all three touchpoints in a single unified transaction, reaching 160+ million listeners at scale — an architecture SiriusXM calls "one buy, one audience."

The standout growth engine is podcasting. Full-year podcast advertising revenue grew 41% in 2025, with advertising revenue overall reaching $1.77 billion for the year and $491 million in Q4, up 3%.

The ad tech powering all of this is AdsWizz — a subsidiary SiriusXM owns outright. AdsWizz is the technology engine powering the monetization of audio content worldwide, and it handles the programmatic infrastructure not just for SiriusXM's own properties but for third-party publishers globally. SiriusXM also expanded its programmatic deal with Amazon DSP in September 2025, giving Amazon advertisers access to SiriusXM's full digital audio portfolio — Pandora and SoundCloud — via the AdsWizz Supply Side Platform, reaching 160 million monthly digital listeners.

---

**The content arms race**

The biggest strategic bet SiriusXM is making is on exclusive premium content to anchor subscriptions and drive podcast ad revenue simultaneously. The spending has been significant:

SiriusXM has acquired exclusive rights for the SmartLess podcast (Jason Bateman, Sean Hayes, Will Arnett) for reportedly over $100 million; Alex Cooper's Unwell Network for $125 million; and deals with Stephen A. Smith and Trevor Noah.

In talk, SiriusXM renewed and expanded relationships with Howard Stern — keeping him exclusively on the platform for three more years — alongside Andy Cohen, Megyn Kelly, and new additions like Stephen A. Smith. On sports, the company extended its NBA partnership and launched the first-ever 24/7 professional wrestling audio channel.

These big podcast deals feed into the larger SiriusXM ecosystem — many have dedicated streaming radio channels with early and exclusive content on the SiriusXM streaming app, a lower-cost $9.99/month subscription designed to bring SiriusXM content to listeners both inside and outside the car and attract younger subscribers.

---

**The core tension**

The fundamental challenge SiriusXM faces is that its subscription business is structurally tied to car sales and an aging demographic, while its growth opportunities are in digital audio and podcasting where Spotify, Apple, and iHeartMedia compete aggressively. The company expects the number of additional paid subscribers to be "modestly lower" in 2026 than in 2025, which is why the advertising growth story — especially podcasting's 41% revenue surge — matters so much. The company is essentially running a slow-decay subscription base while building a fast-growth ad business to compensate. For 2026, management has guided for flat revenue and profit year-over-year, which it's framing as stabilization after several difficult years.
